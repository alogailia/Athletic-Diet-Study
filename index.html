About the Study

Bridging the knowledge gap for fasting athletes

Muslim athletes at every level face a lack of structured, evidence-based guidance for training during Ramadan. This project tracks how fasting affects energy, recovery, hydration, sleep, and training completion — and translates those findings into practical tools coaches and athletes can actually use.

📓
Daily Diary Methodology
Athletes logged 5 quick questions each day — energy, recovery, hydration, sleep, and training completion — over the full 30-day fast.
📱
iPhone Sleep Integration
Participants shared iPhone Health app sleep data at the end of Ramadan, providing objective sleep duration to cross-reference with diary entries.
🤖
AI-Powered Advisor
The Ramadan Advisor app uses study data as context to give personalized training and recovery recommendations based on your sport, intensity, and current week.
📚
Educational Framework
Findings are structured to feed directly into educational materials for athletic programs, coaching staff, and sports medicine teams.
Key Findings

What the data shows

Four consistent patterns emerged across all 12 athletes and 7 sports tracked over the 30-day study period.

📉

Energy dips in Weeks 1–2, then partially recovers

The sharpest drop in self-reported energy occurs in the first two weeks as the body adapts to fasting. By Week 4 athletes show measurable improvement — suggesting physiological adaptation over time.

💧

Dehydration affects nearly half of all training days

42% of all diary entries reported noticeable dehydration. High-intensity athletes (track, soccer, wrestling) hit 58% — reinforcing the need for structured post-iftar hydration windows.

dehydration rate
😴

Fewer than half of athletes met basic sleep thresholds

Only 52% of nights met the 6-hour minimum sleep threshold, driven by suhoor wake-ups and late tarawih schedules. Sleep-adequate days showed recovery scores 0.6 points higher on average.

🔄

Training modification is the dominant coping strategy

61% of sessions were completed as planned; 24% were modified rather than skipped — showing athletes adapted intelligently. Full skips peaked at 20% in Week 2, aligning with the deepest energy dip.
